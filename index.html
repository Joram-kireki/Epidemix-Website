Why IDM

Infectious Disease Modelling

We're working on a project that employs infectious disease modeling, and specificallly, Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) to explore the effectiveness of the Men5CV vaccinein altering the transmission dynamics of meningococcal meningitis. Through comprehensive simulations, the study assesses different vaccination coverage levels, and treatment and the impact on achieving herd immunity by 2030. Infection modelling typicallly involves the use of SIR, one of the most basic models for describing the dynamics of an infectious disease in a population. It compartmentalizes people into three categories. Infection modeling is an eradication effort by The Institute for Disease Modeling (IDM)

WHAT SIR IS:

Susceptible

Individuals who can contract the disease.

Infected

Individuals who have contracted or exposed individuals and can spread the disease.

Recovered

Individuals who have recovered and are now immune from an intervention immunity boost.

Background Context

Meningococcal meningitis continues to pose a significant public health threat across the African meningitis belt, an area characterized by high rates of endemic and epidemic diseases. This region has historically been vulnerable due to its unique climatic and social factors, which facilitate the rapid spread of the disease. Despite the availability of effective vaccines, the ongoing challenge lies in the variable age distribution of meningococcal cases, complicating efforts to control and manage outbreaks efficiently. The recent development and deployment of advanced vaccination strategies offer promising avenues for intervention. However, the effectiveness of these vaccines can vary significantly based on several factors, including coverage rates and the specific demographics of the target population. Therefore, a nuanced understanding of these dynamics is essential for optimizing vaccination strategies. This study aims to employ Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) to rigorously assess and estimate the optimal vaccination coverages required to achieve herd immunity in Nigeria by 2030. Furthermore, it seeks to evaluate the cost-effectiveness of various vaccination strategies, focusing on their potential to adapt to the heterogeneous nature of meningococcal transmission and immunity within the population. Through this approach, the project endeavours to provide actionable insights that could guide public health policy and vaccine deployment strategies, thereby mitigating the impact of meningitis in one of the world's most affected regions.
