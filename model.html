Background Context - Mathematical Modelling

Mathematical and computational models have become increasingly central in epidemiological research, and particularly for infectious diseases. Our solution is a system that bridges the gap between mathematical modelling and real-world problem domains to provide insights into how infection works in a population and gauge how intervention strategies (e.g. vaccination, treatment) can be used in the mix to counter infection. This would be used to make data-driven decisions or influence healthcare or lifestyle choices for individuals, agencies, organizations and even governments. In this context we use Agent-Based Modeling.

Extending SIR to SEIRs, or SVEIDRs

Agent-Based Modelling

Think of Agent-Based Modeling as a system of computational models that simulate behavior of individual agents in order to study emergent phenomena - in terms of disease infection in a population. Agents may represent humans, institutions, microorganisms, and disease vectors and so forth. The agentsâ€™ actions are based on autonomous decision-making and other behavioral traits, implemented through formal rules of interaction in an environment.
By simulating interactions at the individual level, ABMs provide a unique lens through which complex systems can be examined and understood. This modelling approach allows for manipulating numerous variables to create detailed scenarios, offering insights into how different vaccination strategies might perform under various epidemiological and social conditions. For example, ABMs can elucidate how age-specific vaccination thresholds affect herd immunity in localized populations or how interventions can be optimized in response to seasonal outbreak patterns. The strength of ABM lies in its ability to incorporate a diverse range of data inputs, from population mobility patterns to individual health records, thereby producing more realistic and applicable insights for public health strategies. This capability is particularly crucial in regions like the African meningitis belt, where the heterogeneity of population density, health infrastructure, and social practices can significantly alter disease transmission dynamics. Applying ABM in this domain is a promising avenue for achieving a deeper, more operationally relevant understanding of how best to deploy vaccines and other interventions. The ongoing development of such models is essential for tailoring public health responses to the unique challenges posed by meningitis in high-risk regions

Components of a Simulation

Agents: Individual entities with specific characteristics and behaviors.
Environment: The spatial and temporal context in which agents interact.
Rules: The set of instructions governing agent behavior, interactions, and decision-making.
Emergence: The phenomenon where complex system-level patterns and behaviors emerge from the interactions of individual agents.

Extending SIR to SEIRs, or SVEIDRs

Our Methodology

Our runtime framework leverages data from the World Health Organization (WHO) and to simulate the transmission dynamics of meningococcal meningitis in Nigeria. The model's development and simulations were executed using the Starsim Python package, a tool chosen for its robustness in handling complex epidemiological models and flexibility in integrating diverse data types. The core of our simulation model is structured around the SVEIDRS (Susceptible, Vaccinated, Exposed, Infected, Recovered, Susceptible) paradigm, designed to capture the entire lifecycle of meningococcal disease transmission and immunity within a population. This model structure allows for delineating population segments based on their disease status, specifically focusing on vaccinated and recovered individuals, considering their potential for altered susceptibility and infectiousness.
The model incorporates detailed demographic and geographic distributions specific to Nigeria for now, reflecting variations in population density, age structure, and regional health disparities. These demographic layers simulate the localized impact of meningitis and the corresponding efficacy of vaccination programs across different community structures and urban-rural divides.
Key epidemiological parameters, such as contact, infection, and recovery rates, were meticulously derived from WHO reports, peer-reviewed studies, and historical health records. Each parameter was subjected to a rigorous calibration to align the model's output with real-world epidemiological data observed in Nigeria. Calibration involved adjusting these rates to reflect the natural progression of disease under current health interventions and to predict the potential impact of new vaccination strategies. Empirical data were integrated to align the model with current epidemiological trends and healthcare practices. This involved synthesizing data on vaccination coverage, natural immunity levels, and rates of disease transmission within communities obtained from global health databases and national health surveys.

Running the Simulation

The model funtionally runs multiple simulation scenarios in differing contexts to explore a range of vaccination strategies, including varying coverage levels, targeted demographic interventions, and phased rollout plans. These scenarios were designed to assess the threshold levels of vaccination needed to achieve herd immunity by 2030 and to determine the most cost-effective strategies for broad-scale implementation. Implementation of Men5CV significantly reduced the transmission rates and disease incidence across various population segments.
